## What the Values Survey Measures

The values assessment is based on the Portrait Values Questionnaire Revised (PVQ-RR), developed by Shalom Schwartz. It measures 19 basic human values — the motivational goals that guide your decisions, shape your priorities, and define what you consider important in life.

Unlike personality traits, which describe how you behave, values describe what you care about. They are the standards by which you evaluate your own actions and the actions of others.

## The Basic Values

### Self-Direction — Thought

Valuing independent thinking, forming your own opinions, and exploring ideas on your own terms. People high in this value are curious, autonomous thinkers who resist intellectual conformity.

### Self-Direction — Action

Valuing the freedom to choose your own goals and act independently. High scorers prioritise autonomy in how they spend their time and make their choices in daily life.

### Stimulation

Valuing novelty, challenge, and excitement. People high in stimulation seek out new experiences and are energised by variety, risk, and the thrill of the unfamiliar.

### Hedonism

Valuing pleasure and sensory gratification. High scorers enjoy life's pleasures and place importance on enjoying themselves and satisfying physical and emotional desires.

### Achievement

Valuing personal success and demonstrating competence according to social standards. High scorers are ambitious, motivated by accomplishment, and care about being recognised as capable and effective.

### Power — Dominance

Valuing control over people and resources. High scorers want to be in charge, make decisions, and exert influence over others.

### Power — Resources

Valuing material possessions and wealth. High scorers see financial security and having resources as important to their sense of wellbeing.

### Face

Valuing maintaining a positive public image and avoiding shame or humiliation. High scorers care deeply about how they are perceived by others and about protecting their reputation.

### Security — Personal

Valuing safety, health, and stability in your immediate environment. High scorers prioritise keeping themselves and those close to them protected from harm.

### Security — Societal

Valuing stability, order, and security at the societal level. High scorers care about stable institutions, law and order, and predictable social structures.

### Tradition

Valuing respect for customs, cultural heritage, and established practices. High scorers maintain traditional ways of doing things, observe religious or cultural rituals, and accept the role they were born into.

### Conformity — Rules

Valuing compliance with rules, laws, and formal obligations. High scorers believe it is important to follow rules even when no one is watching.

### Conformity — Interpersonal

Valuing politeness and avoiding actions that might upset or harm others. High scorers are careful not to offend and prioritise social harmony in their interactions.

### Humility

Valuing modesty and recognising your own limitations. High scorers do not seek to stand out, believe everyone deserves equal respect, and avoid drawing attention to their achievements.

### Benevolence — Caring

Valuing the welfare of close family and friends. High scorers are loyal, devoted, and motivated by the wellbeing of people in their inner circle.

### Benevolence — Dependability

Valuing being trustworthy and reliable to those close to you. High scorers believe it is important to be someone others can count on.

### Universalism — Concern

Valuing equal treatment and care for all people, including those outside your immediate circle. High scorers are motivated by fairness, social justice, and reducing inequality.

### Universalism — Nature

Valuing the protection of the natural environment. High scorers care about sustainability, environmental preservation, and the relationship between humans and the natural world.

### Universalism — Tolerance

Valuing openness to diverse perspectives, cultures, and ways of life. High scorers embrace difference and reject ethnocentrism.

## Higher-Order Value Groups

The 19 basic values are organised into four higher-order groupings that reflect fundamental motivational tensions.

### Openness to Change

Includes Self-Direction (Thought and Action), Stimulation, and Hedonism. This group captures values centred on autonomy, novelty, and following your own path.

### Self-Enhancement

Includes Achievement, Power (Dominance and Resources), and Face. This group captures values centred on personal success, status, and control over others.

### Conservation

Includes Security (Personal and Societal), Tradition, Conformity (Rules and Interpersonal), and Humility. This group captures values centred on stability, order, and maintaining the status quo.

### Self-Transcendence

Includes Benevolence (Caring and Dependability) and Universalism (Concern, Nature, and Tolerance). This group captures values centred on the welfare of others and the broader world.

## The Values Circumplex

The values form a circular structure (a circumplex) where adjacent values share underlying motivations, and opposite values represent competing priorities. For example:
- **Achievement** and **Benevolence** are in tension — success for yourself can conflict with caring for others.
- **Stimulation** and **Security** are in tension — seeking novelty conflicts with maintaining safety and order.

Understanding where your highest and lowest values fall in this structure helps explain patterns in how you make decisions and what kinds of conflicts you experience.

## Score Interpretation

Scores reflect how important each value is to you relative to the others and compared to the reference population. A high score on Achievement does not mean you are always ambitious — it means that, across a range of situations, this value tends to guide your thinking more than it does for the average person.

Percentiles compare your score to the reference sample. A percentile of 85 on Universalism — Concern means you place more importance on this value than 85% of reference respondents.

## Values and Behaviour

Values are motivations, not guarantees of behaviour. Behaviour also depends on context, habits, and capability. Someone who highly values Conformity may still break rules when their Security or Benevolence values feel more urgently at stake.

Values are most useful for understanding why you make the choices you do, and for spotting tensions when two things you care deeply about pull in opposite directions.
