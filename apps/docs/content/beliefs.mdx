## What the Beliefs Survey Measures

The beliefs assessment measures your primal world beliefs — your deep, largely automatic assumptions about the fundamental nature of the world. These beliefs were identified and studied by Jer Seligman and colleagues, and the assessment is based on the Primals Inventory (PI-99).

Primal world beliefs are distinct from values (what you care about) and personality (how you tend to behave). They describe what you believe the world fundamentally is like.

## The Three Primary Belief Dimensions

### Good vs. Bad

This is the most important primal belief. It reflects whether you see the world as fundamentally good or fundamentally bad. A high score means you see the world as more kind, safe, worthwhile, and full of opportunities. A low score means you see it as more hostile, threatening, and unfair.

This belief predicts life outcomes more strongly than almost any other psychological variable. People with higher Good scores report more life satisfaction, better mental health, more social trust, and greater achievement motivation.

### Enticing vs. Dull

This dimension reflects whether you find the world interesting, beautiful, and worth exploring, or dull, unpleasant, and not worth engaging with. A high score means you experience the world as full of meaning, beauty, and interesting opportunities.

### Safe vs. Dangerous

This dimension reflects whether you see the world as fundamentally stable, fair, and protective, or unstable, unfair, and threatening. A high score means you feel the world is more likely to support and protect you than to harm you.

### Alive vs. Mechanistic

This dimension reflects whether you experience the world as having purposes, agency, and responsiveness — almost as if it cares about what happens — or as a purely mechanical, indifferent system. A high score means you tend to see events as meaningful, intentional, or connected to your own life.

## Secondary Beliefs

The secondary beliefs are more specific aspects of the three primary dimensions.

**Under Enticing:**
- **Meaningful** — Life has deep meaning and purpose.
- **Interesting** — The world is fascinating and worth understanding.
- **Worth Exploring** — There is value in venturing out and discovering new things.
- **Abundant** — Resources, opportunities, and good things exist in sufficient supply.
- **Improvable** — Things can get better; the world is not fixed in its current state.
- **Beautiful** — The world contains genuine beauty worth noticing.
- **Funny** — Life has a humorous dimension; it is possible to find lightness in difficult things.

**Under Safe:**
- **Cooperative** — People generally help rather than harm each other.
- **Harmless** — The world does not actively try to hurt you.
- **Pleasurable** — Life contains genuine pleasure and comfort.
- **Progressing** — Society is generally improving over time.
- **Just** — People tend to get what they deserve; the world is fair.
- **Stable** — The world is predictable and not prone to sudden collapse.
- **Regenerative** — The world has the capacity to recover from damage and setbacks.

**Under Alive:**
- **Needs Me** — The world, or parts of it, actually need your participation and contribution.
- **About Me** — Events in the world are sometimes personally relevant or directed toward you.
- **Intentional** — Things happen for reasons; the world is not purely random.

## Neutral Primals

Some beliefs do not directly feed into the Good vs. Bad dimension and are measured separately:

- **Changing** — The world is in constant flux rather than static.
- **Hierarchical** — The world has natural structures of rank and order.
- **Interconnected** — Everything is linked to everything else.
- **Understandable** — The world is comprehensible and follows knowable rules.
- **Acceptable** — The world as it is can be accepted rather than needing to be changed.

## How Primals Form

Primal world beliefs develop early in life through experience, observation, and cultural transmission. They are shaped by early caregiving environments, safety and stability growing up, and formative positive or negative experiences.

They are resistant to change precisely because they function as foundational filters on all subsequent experience. If you believe the world is dangerous, you will interpret ambiguous events as threats. If you believe it is good, you will interpret the same events as opportunities.

## Primals and Wellbeing

Research consistently shows that people with more positive primal beliefs — especially higher Good scores — have better mental health, stronger relationships, more effective coping with adversity, and greater engagement with life.

This does not mean that positive beliefs are always accurate, or that negative beliefs are always unfounded. For some people, more cautious beliefs reflect real environmental conditions. But the research suggests that, for most people, moderately positive primals are adaptive.

## Primals and Behaviour

Primals shape behaviour without people typically being aware of it. For example:
- Believing the world is **Cooperative** makes you more likely to initiate social contact and trust strangers.
- Believing the world is **Improvable** makes you more likely to put effort into difficult goals.
- Believing the world is **About Me** can increase attention to potential threats but also makes you feel more connected to events around you.

## Interpreting Your Primal Scores

As with personality and values, primal belief scores are relative to a reference population. Your percentile shows where you fall in the distribution — not whether your beliefs are correct.

A low score on Cooperative, for example, does not mean you are wrong about people. It means you are more sceptical of human cooperation than most people in the reference sample. Whether that scepticism is warranted depends on your history and context.

The narrative section of your results highlights which primals are most distinctive for you — either unusually high or unusually low compared to the reference group.
