## What the Beliefs Survey Measures

The beliefs assessment measures your **primal world beliefs** — deep, largely automatic assumptions about the fundamental nature of the world. It is based on the Primals Inventory (PI-99) developed by Jeremy Clifton and colleagues.

Primal world beliefs are distinct from values (what you care about) and personality (how you tend to behave). They describe what you believe the world fundamentally **is** like.

All scales are reported on a 0–50 scale, with percentiles comparing your score to a reference population.

## How the Belief Dashboard Is Organized

The beliefs dashboard presents results in a three-level hierarchy plus a separate "neutral primals" section:

- **Level 1** — One primary belief: **Good vs. Bad**. The single most predictive primal.
- **Level 2** — Three secondary beliefs: **Safe**, **Enticing**, **Alive**. Each rolls up into Good.
- **Level 3** — 22 tertiary beliefs. Each falls under one of the Level 2 dimensions and captures a more specific aspect of how you see the world.
- **Neutral Primals** — Beliefs that don't feed into Good vs. Bad. They describe the world's nature without being "positive" or "negative".

The Level 1 score is shown as a single large gauge. Each Level 2 dimension is shown as a gauge that expands to reveal its tertiary sub-scores. Neutral primals are displayed as a separate section of polar scales.

---

## Level 1 — Primary Belief

### Good vs. Bad

**The most important primal belief.** It reflects whether you see the world as fundamentally good or fundamentally bad — kind, safe, worthwhile, and full of opportunities, versus hostile, threatening, and unfair.

This belief predicts life outcomes more strongly than almost any other psychological variable. People with higher Good scores report more life satisfaction, better mental health, more social trust, and greater achievement motivation.

The Good score is computed as an aggregate of all sub-belief items — it is the umbrella under which Safe, Enticing, and Alive sit.

---

## Level 2 — Secondary Beliefs

Three broad dimensions that together form the Good vs. Bad belief.

### Safe vs. Dangerous

Beliefs about the world being fair, stable, comfortable, and more safe than dangerous. A high score means you feel the world is more likely to support and protect you than to harm you.

### Enticing vs. Dull

How worthwhile, beautiful, meaningful, and interesting the world feels overall. A high score means you experience the world as full of meaning, beauty, and interesting opportunities.

### Alive vs. Mechanistic

The extent to which life is shaped by purpose, participation, and responsiveness. A high score means you tend to see events as meaningful, intentional, or connected to your own life — as opposed to a purely mechanical, indifferent system.

---

## Level 3 — Tertiary Beliefs (22)

Each tertiary belief is a polar scale (low pole vs. high pole) scored 0–50.

### Under Enticing

- **Meaningful** vs. **Meaningless** — The objects and events in our world have deep meaning.
- **Interesting** vs. **Boring** — The world is full of fascinating, intellectually engaging things.
- **Worth Exploring** vs. **Not Worth Exploring** — The world is full of interesting experiences worth discovering.
- **Abundant** vs. **Barren** — The world is full of resources and opportunities.
- **Improvable** vs. **Too Hard to Improve** — Most aspects of the world can be changed for the better.
- **Beautiful** vs. **Ugly** — The world is full of aesthetically interesting, and even beautiful, things.
- **Funny** vs. **Not Funny** — The world offers many opportunities for humour and laughter.

### Under Safe

- **Cooperative** vs. **Competitive** — The world is mostly cooperative and collaborative instead of competitive.
- **Harmless** vs. **Threatening** — Most aspects of the world are not very dangerous.
- **Pleasurable** vs. **Miserable** — Most aspects of the world are hedonically pleasing.
- **Progressing** vs. **Declining** — The world is improving over time rather than getting worse.
- **Just** vs. **Unjust** — The world is generally fair instead of unfair.
- **Stable** vs. **Fragile** — The world is stable and resilient instead of fragile and frail.
- **Regenerative** vs. **Degenerative** — The world tends to repair and heal itself rather than decay.

### Under Alive

- **Needs Me** vs. **Doesn't Need Me** — The world needs you for some specific, perhaps unique, purpose.
- **About Me (Interactive)** vs. **Indifferent** — Circumstances of the world react, in part, based on your influences.
- **Intentional** vs. **Unintentional** — Most things happen for a reason.

---

## Neutral Primals

Beliefs that describe the world's nature without rolling up into Good vs. Bad. Scored on the same 0–50 polar scales.

- **Changing** vs. **Static** — The world is ever-changing instead of constant.
- **Hierarchical** vs. **Nonhierarchical** — Most things in the world can be ordered or ranked.
- **Interconnected** vs. **Separable** — Most aspects of the world are inter-related instead of independent.
- **Understandable** vs. **Too Hard to Understand** — Most aspects of the world can be understood.
- **Acceptable** vs. **Unacceptable** — Most things in the world are best accepted as they are.

## How Scores Are Calculated

Each scale is computed from multiple survey items and reported on a 0–50 scale. Your percentile shows how your score compares to the reference distribution from Clifton's published research.

A percentile of 75 on Cooperative means you scored higher than 75% of reference respondents on that belief.

## How Primals Form

Primal world beliefs develop early in life through experience, observation, and cultural transmission. They are shaped by early caregiving environments, safety and stability growing up, and formative positive or negative experiences.

They are resistant to change precisely because they function as foundational filters on all subsequent experience. If you believe the world is dangerous, you will interpret ambiguous events as threats. If you believe it is good, you will interpret the same events as opportunities.

## Primals and Wellbeing

Research consistently shows that people with more positive primal beliefs — especially higher Good scores — have better mental health, stronger relationships, more effective coping with adversity, and greater engagement with life.

This does not mean that positive beliefs are always accurate, or that negative beliefs are always unfounded. For some people, more cautious beliefs reflect real environmental conditions. But the research suggests that, for most people, moderately positive primals are adaptive.

## Primals and Behaviour

Primals shape behaviour without people typically being aware of it. For example:
- Believing the world is **Cooperative** makes you more likely to initiate social contact and trust strangers.
- Believing the world is **Improvable** makes you more likely to put effort into difficult goals.
- Believing the world is **About Me** can increase attention to potential threats but also makes you feel more connected to events around you.

## Interpreting Your Primal Scores

As with personality and values, primal belief scores are relative to a reference population. Your percentile shows where you fall in the distribution — not whether your beliefs are correct.

A low score on Cooperative, for example, does not mean you are wrong about people. It means you are more sceptical of human cooperation than most people in the reference sample. Whether that scepticism is warranted depends on your history and context.

The narrative section of your results highlights which primals are most distinctive for you — either unusually high or unusually low compared to the reference group.
